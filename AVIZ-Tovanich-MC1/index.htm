Observing that there is a single location for the outbound calls of each node, we consider it to be the origin location of person nodes. We suppose that the communication channels are an important part of the group activity and treat these channels as undirected graphs for simplification.

Excluding graph 4 and 5 with a node-link view

To compare the template graph and the candidate graphs, we created a tool to visualize all node and edge types (except the Demographics channel) on juxtaposed node-link views, with color indicating the inferred location for person nodes. Figure 1 shows that candidate graphs 4 and 5 have a very different structure than the others, with more product nodes and no unconnected person node.


Figure 1: Node-link views for the template graph (top-left) and the five candidate graphs.


Figure 2 shows that like the template graph, candidate graphs 1, 2 and 3 present communities of person nodes structures connected through the communication channels together with nodes that are not connected. While being noticeably sparser than the template, graph 3 remains one of our candidates at this stage together with graph 1 and 2, while we exclude the others.


Figure 2: Node-link views with only the communication channels displayed.



Focusing on the communication channels using graphlets

We conducted a more in-depth analysis of the structure of the communication channels by calculating graphlets on the phone and email edges. Graphlets are “small connected non-isomorphic induced subgraphs of a large network” 1 that help understand local structural similarities in communication structure beyond just counting edges.

We experimented with graphlets of different sizes and settled on 5-node graphlets. 4-node graphlets cannot capture complex connectivities while 6-node graphlets are too numerous to compare. Figure 3 show the graphlet view for the template graph and the three first candidate graphs, a heatmap showing the count of graphlets for each node.


Figure 3: Heatmap of graphlet signature for the communication channels for the template graph and candidate graphs 1, 2 and 3. 5-node graphlets are ordered the same for all graphs and nodes are sorted by Eigenvector centrality per graph.





We observe that the most common graphlet of the template is the clique and the clique minus one , indicating dense communication within the group. Graph 1 tends to have more star-like graphlets , indicating the connection between two separate groups. Graph 2 tends to have clique minus one and bow-tie graphlets , indicating there is a bridge connecting two groups. Graph 3 doesn’t show any strong graphlet patterns since the communication network is sparse. Looking at the heatmap, graph 2 has the most similar communication pattern compared to the template. Next, we chose to validate our hypothesis about graph 2 by looking at the temporal profiles of interactions.

Comparing activities over time for all channels

Figure 4 show the aggregated temporal activities for the template and the three remaining candidate graphs. Edge types are encoded by color and people sorted by their number of activities (edges). It seems that again graph 3 is the most dissimilar. The template graph seems to have two peaks of communication between people during June-July and October-November. Both graphs 1 and 2 also have two peaks but at different times. Graph 2 seems to have more similar temporal activities compared to the template which we further confirmed with the metrics we report in the next question. [498 words, 4 figures]
