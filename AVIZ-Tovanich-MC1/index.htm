Center for Global Cyber Strategy (CGCS) researchers have used the data donated by the white hat groups to create anonymized profiles of the groups. One such profile has been identified by CGCS sociopsychologists as most likely to resemble the structure of the group who accidentally caused this internet outage. You have been asked to examine CGCS records and identify those groups who most closely resemble the identified profile

Observing that there is a single location for the outbound calls of each node, we consider it to be the origin location of person nodes. We suppose that the communication channels are an important part of the group activity and treat these channels as undirected graphs for simplification.

Focusing on the communication channels using graphlets

We conducted a more in-depth analysis of the structure of the communication channels by calculating graphlets on the phone and email edges. Graphlets are “small connected non-isomorphic induced subgraphs of a large network” 1 that help understand local structural similarities in communication structure beyond just counting edges.

We experimented with graphlets of different sizes and settled on 5-node graphlets. 4-node graphlets cannot capture complex connectivities while 6-node graphlets are too numerous to compare. Figure 3 show the graphlet view for the template graph and the three first candidate graphs, a heatmap showing the count of graphlets for each node.


Figure 3: Heatmap of graphlet signature for the communication channels for the template graph and candidate graphs 1, 2 and 3. 5-node graphlets are ordered the same for all graphs and nodes are sorted by Eigenvector centrality per graph.




We observe that the most common graphlet of the template is the clique and the clique minus one , indicating dense communication within the group. Graph 1 tends to have more star-like graphlets , indicating the connection between two separate groups. Graph 2 tends to have clique minus one and bow-tie graphlets , indicating there is a bridge connecting two groups. Graph 3 doesn’t show any strong graphlet patterns since the communication network is sparse. Looking at the heatmap, graph 2 has the most similar communication pattern compared to the template. Next, we chose to validate our hypothesis about graph 2 by looking at the temporal profiles of interactions.

Comparing activities across time for all channels

Figure 4 shows the aggregated temporal activities for the template and the three remaining candidate graphs. Edge types are encoded by color and people sorted by their number of activities (edges).
It seems that again graph 3 is the most dissimilar. The template graph seems to have two peaks of communication between people during June-July and October-November. Both graphs 1 and 2 also have two peaks but at different times. Graph 2 seems to have more similar temporal activities compared to the template which we further confirmed with the metrics we report in the next question. [498 words, 4 figures]

Two features helped us to determine the similarities between the template and candidate graphs: (a) communication network graphlets and (b) the temporal profile of each graph.

Graphlet frequencies confirm our hypothesis about candidate graph 2

We computed graphlet frequencies inside the template and the five candidate graphs, for the 21 undirected 5-node graphlets, on the communication channels. Figure 5 displays the normalized frequencies as bar charts, with bars ordered by decreasing frequencies for the template. 


Figure 5: 5-node graphlet frequencies for the communication channels.




We can see that graph 2 is the most structurally similar candidate with the template graph, with a Pearson's correlation coefficient of 0.761, followed by graph 3 with a correlation of 0.588. Graph 2 and the template share the same most frequent graphlet.


Aggregated temporal profiles further confirm our hypothesis about graph 2

We aggregated the temporal events by counting the number of edges with temporal information for each day and plotted these time series on Figure 6. The most similar candidates were graph 1 and graph 2. Even though none of these graphs present a peak in activity in the middle of the year, graph 1 and graph 2 have a similar pattern by the end of the year with an increase of phone calls and emails, then a high peak and some more activity followed by only travels (in green). However the peak activity from graph 2 is closer in size to the one of the template graph. 


Figure 6: Aggregated temporal series for each channel and each graph.





Using dynamic time warping distance (DTW), we calculated the pairwise distance of the aggregated time series between all graphs and built the hierarchical clustering shown on Figure 7. The visualization of the time series and the result of the hierarchical clustering led us to conclude that graph 2 is the most similar to the template graph.  [291 words, 3 figures]

2 – CGCS has a set of “seed” IDs that may be members of other potential networks that could have been involved. Take a look at the very large graph. Can you determine if those IDs lead to other networks that matches the template? Describe your process and findings in no more than ten images and 500 words.

Our first attempt to answer Question 1 was to pair people according to one similarity measure (e.g. demographics similarity, graphlet counts, time series, travel path). We were unable to find any measure that helped match the template to the candidate graphs suing this strategy (see this interactive tool that allows exploring different person pairing for the candidate graphs based on a single similarity measure). However, we later were successful matching people using demographic and travel profiles in the large graph (explained in detail in Question 3).

Matching template person nodes from the seed nodes

We implemented a greedy matching algorithm to crawl the large graph from the seed nodes. We used the communication channel as it is the only channel which directly connects people. The algorithm proceeds as follows:

  1. From the seed’s person node, we mark the neighbors in the communication channel as candidate nodes.

  2. Every pair of person nodes between the unmatched template nodes and the candidate nodes is compared according to a similarity measure. We select the most similar pair and mark it as a match.

  3. The neighborhood of the newly matched candidate node is appended to the candidate node list.

  4. Repeat step 2 and 3, until each node of the template graph has been matched. At the end, we obtain a set of match for the person nodes of the template graph and induce the corresponding subgraph by extracting all communication edges from the large graph that connect matched nodes, as well as other edge types (i.e. buy, sell, co-authorship, financial, and travel-to).

Similarity measures for person nodes

We tried several measures of similarity and combination of measure of similarity. The demographic similarity is the cosine similarity of two the demographic profiles, that are the vectors of amount spent for each financial categories for each person node. The travel similarity is the Jaccard similarity coefficient of the two sets of trips (source location, target location) of two people. The graphlet-frequencies similarity is a measure derived from the vector of graphlet counts, adjusted for the frequency distribution. We provide more details on it in question 5.


Result

We experimented with three combination of similarity measures to compare person nodes: (1) graphlet, (2) demographics and travels, and (3) demographics, travels, and graphlets. The combination of the demographics similarity and the travel similarity gave the best match. We used only the template nodes which had either demographics or travel information (49 nodes out of the 51 person nodes).



Figure 8: Comparison of the template and the match from seed 1 resulting from a greedy strategy using demographics and travel similarity measures.




Figure 9: Comparison of the template and the match from seed 3 resulting from a greedy strategy using demographics and travel similarity measures.



Figure 8 and 9 show the parallel arc view for the match for the seed 1 (top) and 3 (bottom) using the second strategy (based on both demographics and travels). In the inner tracks, the view shows the similarity of each pair according to the different measures (deep blue is more similar). We see that the similarity between the matched nodes is very high, meaning that the matched nodes have very similar demographic and traveling profiles. On the outer, the view shows the communication links (phone and emails), colored green when both the template and the match agree on an edge, and red or gray when the edge only exists in the template or the match respectively. We can see that a lot of the communication edges are retrieved (green arcs), even if some connections differ.

Since the person node of seed 2 doesn’t have any connections to others, it does not lead to other person nodes to match with the template.
